# Lean vs Coq: implementation difficulty, measured from agent sessions

A comparison of how hard the same refinement proofs were to build in Lean 4 vs
Coq 8.20, based on the recorded agent sessions in [`sessions/`](sessions/README.md)
(token costs, timing splits, transcripts) cross-referenced with git-commit
timestamps. Both campaign targets — hex0 `core_refines` and hex1
`core1_refines` — were proved in **both** systems.

## Method

- Each session's cost/time comes from the per-session headers generated by
  `sessions/gen_sessions.py` (priced from live OpenRouter rates; Claude numbers
  are exact, see the caveats in `sessions/README.md`).
- Sessions are attributed to a system by the git commits that landed inside the
  session's wall-clock window (`lean(...)` / `refine(lean)` vs `coq(...)` /
  `refine(coq)` prefixes). Two sessions were mixed and are split at the commit
  boundary (noted below).
- Failure-mode evidence (OOMs, ulimit caps, compile timeouts) is grepped from
  the transcripts and corroborated by `REFINE1.md`'s gotcha log.

**Important framing:** on both rungs the proof was *developed in Lean first*,
then *ported to Coq* with a complete blueprint (`PROOF.md` for hex0,
`REFINE1.md` + the finished Lean proof for hex1). So the Lean numbers price
*inventing* the proof; the Coq numbers price *transcribing* a known-good one.

## Headline

The Coq ports cost almost as much as the original Lean development despite the
head start — and the difficulty had a different *shape*: Lean spent its budget
on model generation (thinking up proofs; tool feedback near-instant and
reliable), Coq spent it fighting the toolchain (memory blowups, multi-minute
compiles, tactic fragility). Normalized per unit of new proof insight, **Coq
was substantially harder**.

## Where the time went — the telling metric

Model-generation vs tool-execution split per session:

| Session | Work | Model gen | Tool exec |
|---|---|--:|--:|
| #4 (Lean hex0 close) | Lean | 3h11m | 12m40s |
| #11 (Lean hex1 chunks 8–17) | Lean | 1h54m | **45.5s** |
| #5 (Coq hex0 port) | Coq | 1h43m | 6m36s |
| #12 (Coq hex1 chunks 1–7) | Coq | 1h20m | **40m52s** |
| #15 (Coq hex1 chunks 8–13b) | Coq | 1h28m | **2h52m** (three 10-min compile timeouts) |
| #16 (Coq hex1 finish) | Coq | 50m | 17m18s |

Lean's `lake env lean` feedback loop was near-instant: the entire 10-chunk Lean
hex1 session #11 spent **under a minute** in tools. The Coq hex1 port spent
**~4h in coqc alone**, with individual proofs taking 10+ minutes or dying
outright.

## Failure-mode evidence

- **OOM/killed/ulimit mentions in transcripts:** Coq sessions #15 (122) and
  #16 (234, plus 11 `ulimit` references) dominate; Lean-only sessions have
  0–8. The hex1 Coq port required capping coqc at **16 GiB via ulimit**
  (`Makefile.local` wrapper), ~60 throwaway `Oom*.v` experiments, and a custom
  `clia` tactic — bare `lia` in State-chain contexts OOM'd at the 16 GiB cap,
  while `clia`-fixed `p2_ref` compiles in 171 s / 1.0 GB.
- **Session attrition:** the Coq hex1 port killed a tmux session (#15 opens
  with *"why did the previous session die in tmux?"*) and stretched over
  3 calendar days vs Lean's single-day 17-chunk run.
- **Gotcha inventory** (`REFINE1.md`): Coq's list is long and resource-fatal —
  Equations `simp f in hyp` with an IH in context → 128 GB OOM (use
  `autorewrite`), `simpl` divergence on big-literal Nat division, the lazy
  `eq_refl` trap in `repeat split` under vm_compute, Z-vs-nat intermediate
  blowups. Lean's list is short and benign — `omega` pulling
  `Classical.choice` on conjunction goals, the lake build-root quirk,
  no-Mathlib lemma gaps.

## Proof size — difficulty per line was comparable

| Artifact | Lean (LoC) | Coq (LoC) |
|---|--:|--:|
| hex0 Refine | 3,377 | 3,322 |
| hex1 Refine (+ base/decode-facts) | 9,564 | 7,717 |

Nearly 1:1 — neither system needed materially more proof text. The difficulty
gap is not verbosity; it is **toolchain resource behavior and tactic
fragility**.

## Verdict & caveats

**Coq was the harder system here**: a blueprinted port cost ≈90–95% of original
development and 3× the calendar time on hex1, with all the catastrophic
failures (OOMs, dead sessions, capped builds) on the Coq side.

Confounds to keep honest:

1. **Ordering** — Coq always went second, so its proofs were Lean-shaped;
   porting friction (Equations vs structural recursion, Z vs Nat conventions)
   inflates its numbers even as the blueprint deflates them.
2. **Agent skill prior** — a single agent (claude-opus-4-8) did both sides; any
   asymmetry in its Lean vs Coq proficiency is folded into the numbers.
3. **Noisy metrics excluded** — raw `error`-keyword counts in transcripts are
   unusable here because "error" is part of the hex spec domain
   (`error_result`, error-case lemmas).
